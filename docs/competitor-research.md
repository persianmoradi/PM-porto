# Phase 3 — Competitor & Best-Practice Research

_Date: 2026-06-25. Sources listed at end._

## The core tension in the data

Two truths sit on top of each other. Award sites (Awwwards, studio/agency work) win on
**expressive craft** — motion, 3D, bold type, signature interactions. Hiring data says the
opposite: **"a clean, fast, readable portfolio beats a flashy slow one every time"**,
because hiring managers scan and want the work, not a loading animation. Your locked
strategy — *minimal canvas, bold signature moments* — is precisely the resolution of this
tension, and the research validates it. You get the craft signal without paying the
speed/credibility tax.

## Layout patterns that recur in the best portfolios

The strongest sites are short. Three to five projects is the repeated sweet spot — enough
to show range, few enough that each gets real attention. The structure that converts:
a sharp hero that states who you are in one line, two or three deep case studies, a
lightweight "more work" tier, an about section with a human face and a clear skills list,
and a single unmissable contact CTA. Engineer-designer sites (Fine Thought / Nathan Leigh
Davis, Lazy Eight) lean on disciplined grid systems and typographic detail rather than
decoration — the grid *is* the design.

## Typography systems

Across the Typewolf-featured portfolios, type does the heavy lifting: one characterful
display face paired with a highly legible body face, a deliberate modular scale, and
generous spacing. This is doubly important for you because minimalism leaves type nowhere
to hide — and your `CLAUDE.md` already bans the generic defaults (Inter/Roboto/etc.). The
type choice will be the single most identity-defining decision in Phase 4.

## Animation / motion systems

Award winners use motion as *narrative* (reveal, focus, transition between states), not
ambient decoration. The pattern to copy: a few flawless signature moments — a hero reveal,
one memorable project transition, considered scroll-linked focus — with everything else
calm. Critically for a design engineer, the craft is judged on the *details*: easing
curves, timing, and respecting `prefers-reduced-motion`. Sloppy motion reads worse than no
motion.

## Storytelling & case studies

This is the single biggest junior/senior separator in the data: top 2–3 projects need
real **case studies**, not screenshots. The winning formula is context → problem → what
you built → outcome. The example that keeps appearing: not "here's a todo app" but "I
built this because every existing tool was too complex for my workflow; it does X; N
people use it monthly." Even for placeholder/early projects, write them in this shape — the
*narrative structure* signals product thinking even before the metrics exist.

## Conversion & trust

Conversion is won by removing friction: one primary CTA (yours is "hire me"), repeated
unobtrusively, with email/LinkedIn/GitHub one click away. Trust is built through proof of
recency (stale projects actively hurt — "if your latest project is from 2024 it sends a
bad signal"), a real photo/identity, and visible craft. For you, the site's own
performance and polish *is* a trust signal — a fast, accessible, beautifully built site
proves the claim "I'm a frontend/design engineer" better than any sentence.

## Where you can outperform

1. **The site as the case study.** Most students show projects; few make the site itself
   the headline proof. A flawless, accessible, fast build with one signature interaction
   beats ten mediocre project cards.
2. **Honest, well-structured early work.** Writing placeholder/learning projects in real
   case-study shape (problem→build→outcome) signals maturity most juniors lack.
3. **AI-fluency shown, not stated.** A small, genuinely useful LLM-powered feature
   (woven in, per your positioning) is a concrete differentiator vs. portfolios that just
   list "AI" as a skill.
4. **Recency as a system.** Architecture that makes adding a case study trivial keeps the
   site perpetually current — directly countering the #1 trust-killer in the data.
